Offline reinforcement learning makes it possible to train policies from a fixed
trajectory dataset, but it also creates a verification problem: a model producer
may claim that temporal-difference computations were performed over a particular
dataset without revealing the raw trajectories or intermediate model values. We
study this problem for offline Deep Q-Network (DQN) training over committed
trajectory data. The contribution is intentionally scoped: we do not claim a
full proof of DQN training. Instead, we build a reproducible verification
pipeline whose cryptographic backend proves the correctness of the
reinforcement-learning-specific temporal-difference (TD) computation.

The system commits an offline CartPole transition dataset with a Merkle tree,
exports canonical fixed-point TD witnesses, and implements an SP1 zkVM backend
for single-transition and minibatch TD statements. The proved relation checks
transition membership in the committed dataset, canonical leaf hashing, Merkle
path recomputation, terminal and non-terminal Bellman target semantics, TD error
recomputation, SmoothL1 loss recomputation, and public target/loss or
batch-loss consistency. A Python verifier is retained as the semantic oracle and
as a pre-ZK extension path for one-step SGD update checks and short trace
chaining.

In the locked benchmark, SP1 proves TD-1, TD-2, TD-4, and TD-8 cases with proof
sizes from 2.78 MB to 2.81 MB and proving times from 142.32 s to 275.08 s on
the recorded Kaggle run. Verification time is approximately 0.16 s for all four
cases. Python and SP1 agree on the valid fixtures and on an adversarial suite
covering schema, reward, rounding, done-branch, Merkle path, target-value,
claimed-output, and batch-aggregation tampering. These results demonstrate a
working ZK-backed TD verification pipeline for offline DQN over committed
trajectories, while making explicit that neural-network forward passes,
gradients, optimizer updates, checkpoint-to-checkpoint transitions, and full
proof-of-training remain outside the current backend.

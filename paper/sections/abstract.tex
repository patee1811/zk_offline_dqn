\begin{abstract}
We study a concrete pathway toward verifiable reinforcement learning by focusing on a narrow and auditable setting: offline Deep Q-Network (DQN) training from a fixed trajectory dataset. Rather than attempting to address general ``RL + ZK'' at once, we isolate an experimental pipeline in which trajectories are first collected, then flattened into a fixed transition dataset, committed via a Merkle-style structure, and finally used for offline value learning. This design matches the requirements of commitment, reproducibility, and proof-oriented statement design.

Our experiments on CartPole-v1 reveal that dataset structure strongly interacts with the learning paradigm. On a narrow, high-return behavior dataset collected from a trained DQN policy with $\epsilon=0.10$, naive offline Double DQN collapses, with 100-episode re-evaluation ranging only from 31.50 to 94.68 across seeds. In contrast, behavior cloning on the same dataset is strong, reaching 392.87, 461.88, and 398.19 mean return over 100 evaluation episodes across three seeds. Conservative Q-Learning (CQL-lite) substantially improves over naive offline Double DQN on the same dataset, reaching 416.14, 341.79, and 447.42 mean return.

Motivated by these results, we implement a pre-ZK artifact-verifier prototype for the core computations that a future proof system would need to certify. The current prototype verifies membership of sampled transitions in a committed dataset, Double-DQN-style Bellman target correctness, SmoothL1 temporal-difference loss correctness, minibatch-average loss correctness, and public anchoring of the audited checkpoint via SHA-256. While this is not yet a full zero-knowledge proof of training, it establishes the concrete statement structure required for such a system and identifies verified TD arithmetic on committed offline samples as the correct first milestone before attempting proofs of gradient updates.
\end{abstract}

\begin{abstract}
We study a concrete pathway toward zero-knowledge verifiable reinforcement learning by focusing on a narrow and auditable setting: offline Deep Q-Network (DQN) training from a fixed trajectory dataset. Rather than addressing general ``RL + ZK'' at once, we isolate an experimental pipeline in which trajectories are first collected, then flattened into a fixed transition dataset, and finally used for offline value learning. This design matches the needs of commitment, reproducibility, and proof generation.

Our experiments on CartPole-v1 reveal that dataset structure strongly interacts with the learning paradigm. On a narrow, high-return behavior dataset collected from a trained DQN policy with $\epsilon=0.10$, naive offline Double DQN collapses, with 100-episode re-evaluation ranging only from 31.50 to 94.68 across seeds. In contrast, behavior cloning on the same dataset is strong, reaching 392.87, 461.88, and 398.19 mean return over 100 evaluation episodes across three seeds. Conservative Q-Learning (CQL-lite) substantially improves over naive offline Double DQN on the same dataset, reaching 416.14, 341.79, and 447.42 mean return.

These findings support two claims. First, high-quality narrow behavior data is suitable for imitation but problematic for naive bootstrapped offline Q-learning. Second, conservative offline RL objectives provide a promising middle ground between imitation learning and unstable offline temporal-difference learning. Based on these results, we propose a zero-knowledge roadmap that begins with proving minibatch membership in a committed dataset together with correct Bellman target and temporal-difference loss computation, before considering proofs of gradient updates.
\end{abstract}

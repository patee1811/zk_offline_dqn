We presented a concrete research path toward verifiable offline DQN training from committed data. Instead of attempting to solve general RL verifiability at once, we first established an offline benchmark pipeline on CartPole-v1, studied how different datasets interact with different learning paradigms, and then implemented a pre-ZK artifact-verifier prototype for the core TD computations of interest.

Our empirical findings are clear. A narrow high-return behavior dataset is excellent for behavior cloning, but naive offline Double DQN collapses on it. A conservative Q-learning objective substantially recovers performance, making it a stronger candidate for future verifiable training than naive offline offline Q-learning on narrow behavior data. Conversely, broader-coverage data is much more favorable to offline bootstrapped value learning, highlighting the importance of dataset structure for any meaningful verifiability claim.

These observations matter directly for the verification agenda. Verifiability is only useful if the certified computation is itself meaningful, stable, and tied to a fixed committed dataset. Accordingly, the present repository now supports verification of committed-sample membership, Double-DQN-style Bellman target correctness, SmoothL1 TD loss correctness, minibatch-average loss correctness, and public checkpoint anchoring through SHA-256. This is still weaker than a full proof of training, since it does not yet certify optimizer updates, target-network synchronization across training steps, or the derivation of the checkpoint itself from a sequence of correct updates.

The next step is therefore not ``general RL + ZK,'' but one verified offline DQN update step. That stronger milestone will require formalizing public and private inputs for pre-update and post-update checkpoints, representing gradients and optimizer arithmetic in a proof-friendly form, and extending the current committed-data and TD-arithmetic prototype into a genuine proving backend.

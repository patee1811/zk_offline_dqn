We presented a concrete research path toward zero-knowledge verifiable offline DQN training from committed trajectories. Instead of attempting to solve general RL verifiability at once, we first established an offline benchmark pipeline on CartPole-v1 and studied how different datasets interact with different learning paradigms.

Our findings are clear. A narrow high-return behavior dataset is excellent for behavior cloning, but naive offline Double DQN collapses on it. A conservative Q-learning objective substantially recovers performance, making it a much stronger candidate for future verifiable training than naive offline Q-learning. Conversely, a broad random dataset can support strong offline Double DQN performance, highlighting the importance of dataset coverage.

These observations matter for the zero-knowledge agenda. Verifiability is only useful if the certified computation is itself meaningful and stable. The present results therefore motivate a proof roadmap that begins with committed minibatch membership, Bellman target correctness, and TD loss correctness over fixed offline datasets, and only later extends to gradient update proofs.

The next steps are to formalize dataset commitments, define precise public and private inputs for the MVP proof statement, and translate the experimentally validated offline training computations into proof-friendly arithmetic circuits.

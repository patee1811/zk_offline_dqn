Reinforcement learning (RL) systems~\cite{mnih2015dqn} are increasingly used in settings where the correctness of training may matter, but their learning pipelines are difficult to audit. This problem becomes especially acute when training is performed over trajectories collected elsewhere, when datasets are reused, or when an external party must trust that a claimed update was actually computed from a fixed dataset. Zero-knowledge proof systems~\cite{thalerbook} offer a potential route toward verifiable training, but directly proving general RL training is currently too broad and technically expensive.

This paper adopts a narrower and more structured objective: zero-knowledge verifiable offline DQN training from committed trajectories. The central design choice is to avoid general online RL and instead focus on offline training~\cite{levine2020offline} over a fixed dataset. This choice is motivated by three requirements. First, fixed datasets can be committed once and reused, for example through a Merkle tree~\cite{merkle1987}. Second, offline training is reproducible in a way that online exploration is not. Third, fixed training steps admit precise arithmetic statements that can be expressed inside proof systems~\cite{groth2016zksnark}.

Our work follows a staged methodology. We first build an end-to-end experimental pipeline for collecting trajectories, flattening them into transitions, and training offline baselines on CartPole-v1~\cite{brockman2016gym}. We then analyze how different dataset structures interact with different learning paradigms, including naive offline Double DQN~\cite{hasselt2016double}, behavior cloning, and Conservative Q-Learning~\cite{kumar2020cql}. Finally, we use these findings to define a realistic zero-knowledge roadmap that begins with proof statements for dataset membership, Bellman target computation, and temporal-difference (TD) loss, rather than attempting to prove full RL training at once.

The experimental results already reveal a strong and useful pattern. On a narrow behavior dataset collected from a trained DQN policy, behavior cloning performs well, while naive offline Double DQN collapses. A conservative offline RL objective substantially recovers performance. Conversely, a broad random dataset can support strong offline Double DQN performance, albeit with large seed sensitivity. These observations suggest that the eventual verifiable training target should not merely reproduce any offline RL algorithm, but should instead focus on algorithms whose training dynamics are stable enough to be meaningfully audited.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item We define a concrete research direction for zero-knowledge verifiable offline DQN training from committed trajectories.
    \item We build an initial benchmark pipeline on CartPole-v1, including dataset generation, offline baselines, checkpoint selection, and multi-seed evaluation.
    \item We show experimentally that dataset suitability depends strongly on the learning paradigm: imitation, naive offline Q-learning, and conservative offline RL behave very differently on the same fixed dataset.
    \item We implement a pre-ZK artifact-verifier prototype that verifies committed minibatch membership, Bellman target computation, TD loss correctness, and one-step SGD update consistency, establishing the concrete statement structure required for a future proof system.
\end{itemize}

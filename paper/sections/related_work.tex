\paragraph{Deep reinforcement learning.}
The Deep Q-Network (DQN) algorithm~\cite{mnih2015dqn} demonstrated that deep neural networks can learn effective control policies through temporal-difference learning~\cite{watkins1992qlearning} with experience replay and a target network. A key refinement, Double DQN~\cite{hasselt2016double}, addresses overestimation bias by decoupling action selection from value evaluation: the online network selects the best next action, while the target network evaluates that action. Our system uses Double DQN as the primary value-learning architecture for offline training, making this decoupled structure a core part of the verification statement.

\paragraph{Offline reinforcement learning.}
Offline RL~\cite{levine2020offline} addresses the problem of learning policies from a fixed dataset without further environment interaction. A central challenge is distribution shift: the learned policy may select actions poorly represented in the data, leading to extrapolation errors. Fujimoto et al.~\cite{fujimoto2019offpolicy} showed that naive off-policy methods fail in the purely offline setting. Conservative Q-Learning (CQL)~\cite{kumar2020cql} mitigates this by regularizing the Q-function to produce lower-bound value estimates on out-of-distribution actions. Our work evaluates both naive offline Double DQN and a CQL-lite variant on committed datasets, finding that the conservative objective is essential for stable value learning on narrow behavior-generated data.

\paragraph{Verifiable machine learning.}
The notion of verifying that a machine learning model was correctly trained has been explored under the concept of Proof-of-Learning~\cite{jia2021proofoflearning}, which aims to provide evidence that computational work was performed during training. Our approach differs in that we target cryptographically grounded verification of specific training computations---Bellman target computation, temporal-difference loss, and individual SGD updates---over committed data, rather than statistical evidence of training effort.

\paragraph{Zero-knowledge proof systems.}
Zero-knowledge proofs allow a prover to convince a verifier of a statement's truth without revealing the underlying witness~\cite{thalerbook}. Modern constructions such as zkSNARKs~\cite{groth2016zksnark} and PLONK~\cite{gabizon2019plonk} enable succinct, non-interactive verification of general computations expressed as arithmetic circuits. While our current implementation is a pre-ZK artifact-verifier prototype rather than a true zero-knowledge system, the statement structure and fixed-point arithmetic are designed to be compatible with future deployment on such proving backends.

\paragraph{Cryptographic commitments.}
Merkle trees~\cite{merkle1987} provide an efficient mechanism for committing to a set of data elements and later proving membership of individual elements through authentication paths. In our system, the offline transition dataset is committed using a Merkle tree, and sampled transitions used for training are verified against the public root hash. This commitment structure ensures that the verifier can confirm that training computations are performed over the genuine committed dataset rather than adversarially chosen samples.
